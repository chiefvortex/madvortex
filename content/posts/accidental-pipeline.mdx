I didn't plan to build a studio. I just kept saying yes to projects that scared me, and eventually I needed help.

That's the honest version. The narrative I tell clients sounds more strategic: "identified a gap in the market," "built systems to scale quality," etc. That's true too, but it came after. The pipeline came first, accidentally.

## The First Yes

In 2019, I said yes to a project I couldn't do alone. A brand film that needed VFX, color, sound design, and motion graphics. I knew VFX. The rest? I knew people who knew people.

I called in favors. A colorist I'd assisted once. A sound designer from a forum. A motion artist I'd never met in person. We figured it out on WhatsApp groups and late-night calls.

The project was messy. Handoffs were chaotic. Nobody used the same software or file formats. But we delivered something good. The client was happy. They came back with a bigger project.

## Systems Emerge

The second project needed the same mix of skills, but I couldn't run it the same way. The WhatsApp chaos almost killed us the first time. So I built a Notion database. Nothing fancy—just tracking who had what file, what was pending, what was approved.

That database became a template. The template became a system. The system became a studio.

I didn't set out to build infrastructure. I built it because I needed it to survive. Every piece of the Vortex pipeline exists because at some point, I was drowning and had to build a life raft.

## The People Problem

The hardest part of scaling wasn't technical. It was people.

Finding artists who match your quality bar is hard. Finding artists who match your quality bar *and* can work asynchronously with strangers in different time zones *and* can handle direct client feedback *and* won't ghost you mid-project? That's nearly impossible.

I've been burned. Multiple times. Projects that went sideways because someone disappeared, or delivered substandard work, or just... stopped responding.

Now I have a core team. People I've worked with for years. People I trust with hero shots and tight deadlines. Building that trust took failures. Expensive failures.

## What I Learned

The pipeline is never finished. Every new project type, every new client requirement, every new tool—they all require adaptation. The system I have now is version 47 of the system I started with.

Flexibility matters more than perfection. A "good enough" process that actually gets used beats an "optimal" process that nobody follows.

Documentation is a gift to your future self. Every time I write down how something works, future-me sends thanks back through time.

## The Accidental Part

Here's what makes it accidental: I never wanted to be a studio founder. I wanted to make things. I still want to make things. The studio exists to support the making.

Some people optimize for scale. I optimize for quality and sanity. The pipeline grew to serve the work, not the other way around.

If I'd tried to plan all this from the beginning, I would have failed. I would have built systems for problems I didn't understand yet. Instead, I built systems for problems I was actively experiencing.

That's the accidental pipeline: solutions to real problems, accumulated over time, eventually resembling something intentional.

It works. Not because I planned it. Because I survived it.
