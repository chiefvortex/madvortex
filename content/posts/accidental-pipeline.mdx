It started the way most good things do: with a deadline I had no business agreeing to.

A client needed forty shots composited in two weeks. Our usual timeline for that volume is six weeks. I said yes because I'm an optimist with poor time management skills, and then I spent the next seventy-two hours figuring out how to not embarrass myself.

## The experiment

I started feeding our reference footage into every AI tool I could find. Not as a replacement for the work, but as a first pass -- a rough draft that our compositors could refine instead of build from scratch.

The results were terrible. Then they were okay. Then, around the twenty-hour mark of not sleeping, something clicked. The AI wasn't giving us finished work. It was giving us a starting point that was 60% of the way there. And that last 40%? That's where the craft lives anyway.

## What we built

Over the next week, we stitched together a workflow that looked nothing like a traditional VFX pipeline. AI handled the heavy lifting -- rough keys, initial color matching, basic stabilization. Our artists handled the decisions -- the timing, the feel, the moments that make a shot land instead of just exist.

We delivered on time. The client couldn't tell which shots used AI assistance and which didn't. That was the point.

## The lesson

The best tools are invisible. They don't announce themselves. They don't make you feel like you're using technology -- they make you feel like you're just working faster.

That accidental pipeline is now how we work at Vortex. Not because AI replaced anyone, but because it gave everyone on the team more time to do the thing they're actually good at: making decisions that machines can't.
