The tools I learned on will be museum pieces. The workflows we debate today will be automated. And most VFX artists aren't ready for what's coming.

I've been doing this for over a decade. Started with After Effects tutorials on YouTube, moved to Nuke, learned the pipeline, worked on commercials, features, web3 projects. The standard trajectory. But what I'm seeing now—the rate of change in AI-assisted production—makes everything before feel like prologue.

## The Death of Rotoscoping

Let's start with the obvious. Rotoscoping, as a dedicated discipline, has maybe two years left. Not because the work isn't needed—isolation will always be part of the process—but because AI is already doing it better than most junior artists.

I tested five different AI roto tools last month. Three of them produced results that would have taken a skilled roto artist 4-6 hours in under 10 minutes. And they're getting better every week.

This isn't speculation. This is math.

## The Real Disruption: Context Understanding

But rotoscoping is the easy example. Everyone sees that coming. What's less obvious is how AI will change the *creative* side of VFX.

The models being trained right now don't just understand pixels—they understand scenes. They know what a car is. They know how light behaves on metal. They know that when a character walks behind a lamp post, they should reappear on the other side.

This context understanding changes everything. We're moving from pixel manipulation to scene manipulation. From "paint this out frame by frame" to "remove this character from the scene and make it look like they were never there."

## What Survives

Not everything dies. Art direction survives. Taste survives. The ability to look at a shot and know what's wrong with it—that's a human skill that AI amplifies rather than replaces.

The VFX supervisor role actually gets more important, not less. Someone needs to direct these tools, to understand their limitations, to know when the AI output is good enough and when it needs human intervention.

But the ratio shifts dramatically. Where you once needed 50 artists, you might need 5. Those 5 better be really, really good.

## My Bet

I'm betting on the intersection of technical skill and creative vision. Artists who can write prompts *and* know color theory. Supervisors who understand both machine learning limitations and camera optics.

The specialist who only knows rotoscoping is in trouble. The generalist who understands the full pipeline and can direct AI tools? They're going to thrive.

This isn't pessimism. This is adaptation. The industry is changing whether we like it or not. The only question is whether you're positioned to ride the wave or get pulled under.

I know which one I'm choosing.
